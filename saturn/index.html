  • Saturn gets its name from the Roman god of agriculture.
  • NASA's probe named Cassini is currently orbiting and studying Saturn.
  • Cassini will continue studying Saturn until
    September 15, 2017.

Facts About Saturn

  • Its rings are not solid. They are made up of bits of ice, dust, and rock. These bits can be anywhere from as small as a grain of sand to more than half-a-mile across.
  • Saturn has 62 known moons orbiting it. When Galileo discovered Saturn in 1610, he thought its rings were actually two moons. In 1655, Dutch astronomer Christian Huygens determined they were rings, not moons, using a more advanced telescope.

You cannot stand on Saturn. It is not like Earth. Saturn is made mostly of gases. It has a lot of helium, the same kind of gas that you put in balloons. This means we can only take pictures of the outer layer of these gasses.

The gas Saturn is made of is a lot like Jupiter, but much less interesting to look at. But enhanced-color images shows the bands which run parallel to the equator, showing the violent winds within the atmosphere. The enchanced color images also show differences in the planet's composition.

Saturn's Hexagon

Saturn's hexagon is at its north pole. Each of the hexagon's sides is larger than the diameter of Earth. The hexagon is constantly rotating, but never moves.

Scroll down to learn more.

The hexagon is a collection of turbulet airflow that results from the high winds at Saturn's equator.

The hexagon was first discovered by NASA's two Voyager probes as they passed Saturn in the early 1980s. NASA's Cassini probe arrived at Saturn in 2004 and was able to study the hexagon through 2009.

Saturn's Rings

  • Other planets in our solar system have rings, but Saturn's rings are the largest by far.
  • The rings are rather thin compared to the planet as a whole. Saturn is around 60,000 kilometers thick while the rings can be as thin as only 10 meters and as large as 1 kilometer thick.
  • Data from the Cassini space probe indicates that the rings of Saturn possess their own own atmosphere that is independent of the planet itself.

Saturn's Moons

Saturn has over 60 moons that range in size from smaller than a football stadium to larger than the planet Mercury.

The larger moons are spherical, but others are shaped like sweet potatoes, meatballs, or sponges.

Hyperion

Hyperion was discovered in 1848 by William Cranch Bond, George Phillips Bond, and William Lassell. It is distinguished by its irregular shape, a chaotic rotation, and unexplained sponge-like appearance. It was the first non-round moon to ever be discovered.

Enceladus

Enceladus is a small ocean world covered in ice. Heat from below the moon's crust causes jets of water vapor and ice particles will erupt from Enceladus' underground ocean through deep crevasses near the south pole. Those fast moving ice particles escape from Enceladus and help replenish part of Saturn's rings.

With its global ocean, unique chemistry, and internal heat, Enceladus has become a promising lead in our search for worlds where life could exist.

Titan

Before 2004, we knew very little about Titan other than its size and that it had a dense, nitrogen-rich atmosphere. Data from Cassini and the Huygens Titan probe revealed that the moon has lakes and seas of liquid methane and ethane, replenished by rain from hydrocarbon clouds. The mission also provided evidence that Titan is hiding an internal, liquid ocean beneath its surface, likely composed of water and ammonia.
